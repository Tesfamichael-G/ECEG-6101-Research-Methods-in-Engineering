1. Ethics vs. Norms

Ethics: The branch of philosophy dealing with values relating to human conduct, with respect to the rightness and wrongness of certain actions and to the goodness and badness of the motives and ends of such actions.
Ethical Norms: The specific "body of principles" or rules that govern behavior in a professional context. In research, these are codified by institutions (like IEEE or University Senates).

Why do we need them?

  • To ensure truth-seeking (science relies on trust).
  • To protect human/animal subjects.
  • To maintain public confidence in engineering.

2. Conducting Research: Data Integrity

The core of engineering ethics is honesty in data collection, analysis, and interpretation. There are three major sins in research, often called FFP.

🚫 Fabrication

Making up data or results.

Example: You didn't run the simulation, but you generated random numbers that look like a valid BER curve and reported them as real results.

🚫 Falsification

Manipulating research materials, equipment, or processes, or changing/omitting data.

Example: You ran the simulation 10 times. 9 times it failed, 1 time it worked. You only report the 1 success and hide the 9 failures. This is "Cherry-Picking."

🚫 Plagiarism

The appropriation of another person's ideas, processes, results, or words without giving appropriate credit.

Example: Copying a paragraph from an IEEE paper into your thesis without quotation marks or citation. Or, copying code from GitHub without attribution.

✅ Best Practice: Record Keeping Keep a detailed lab notebook (digital or physical). Record every simulation seed, every parameter change, and every failed attempt. This proves your work is authentic.

3. Authorship, Copyright & Patenting

A. Authorship Criteria

Who gets to be an author on a paper? According to IEEE and international standards, authorship requires ALL of the following:

  1. Substantial contributions to the conception or design of the work; OR the acquisition, analysis, or interpretation of data.
  2. Drafting the work or revising it critically for important intellectual content.
  3. Final approval of the version to be published.
  4. Agreement to be accountable for all aspects of the work.

Note: Providing funding or general supervision alone does NOT qualify someone for authorship (they go in Acknowledgements).

B. Copyright

Copyright protects the expression of an idea (the text, the figures, the code), not the idea itself.

  • When you publish in an IEEE journal, you often transfer copyright to IEEE.
  • You cannot simply copy-paste your own published paper into your thesis without checking the publisher's policy (usually allowed for thesis, but must cite).

C. Patenting

Patents protect inventions (new processes, machines, compositions of matter).

  • Public Disclosure Bar: If you publish your research (paper/conference) BEFORE filing a patent, you may lose the right to patent it in many countries. Always consult your university's Technology Transfer Office before publishing if your work has commercial potential.

4. Data Sharing & Management

Modern science emphasizes Open Science. Researchers are increasingly expected to share their data and code.

Principle Guideline
FAIR Data Data should be Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable.
Privacy If your data involves humans (e.g., user surveys, location data), you MUST anonymize it to protect identity (GDPR compliance).
Retention Keep raw data for at least 3-5 years after publication in case of audits or questions.

5. Confidentiality in Peer Review

As a researcher, you will eventually review papers for journals. This is a privilege that comes with strict ethical duties.

  • Confidentiality: The manuscript is a private document. You cannot share it with colleagues, students, or friends without the editor's permission.
  • No Conflict of Interest: If you know the author, or are competing with them, you must declare it and recuse yourself.
  • No Misappropriation: You cannot use ideas from a paper you are reviewing to advance your own work before it is published. This is theft of intellectual property.
  • Objectivity: Reviews should be constructive and based on technical merit, not personal bias.
✅ The Golden Rule of Ethics: Treat other researchers' work with the same respect and integrity you expect for your own.
