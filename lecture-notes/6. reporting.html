Chapter 6: Reporting Research Findings

The IMRaD Structure

  • Introduction ("Here's the Problem")
  • Methods ("Here's How I Studied It")
  • Results ("Here's What I Found")
  • Discussion ("Here's What It Means")
  • Oral Presentation Tips

1. Introduction

"Here is the problem, and why you should care."

The Introduction sets the stage. It moves from general context to your specific research question (the "Funnel" approach).

Key Components:
  • Hook/Context: Briefly introduce the broad field (e.g., "5G networks require ultra-low latency...").
  • Problem Statement: Identify the specific gap or issue (e.g., "...but current handover algorithms cause delays >50ms.").
  • Objective: State clearly what this report/paper achieves (e.g., "This study proposes a new predictive handover algorithm...").
  • Roadmap: Briefly outline the rest of the paper.

1.1 The Problem Statement

The problem statement is the most important paragraph in your proposal. If the problem isn't clear, the solution doesn't matter. Use the Hook-Gap-Goal framework.

The Hook-Gap-Goal Framework

  1. The Hook (Context): What is the general situation? Why is this field important?
    "5G networks promise ultra-low latency for critical applications..."
  2. The Gap (The Problem): What is missing, broken, or unknown?
    "...however, current handover algorithms fail to maintain connectivity under high-mobility conditions (>100 km/h), causing packet loss."
  3. The Goal (Your Solution): What will you do to fill the gap?
    "This research proposes a predictive handover algorithm using LSTM networks to reduce packet loss by 20%."
💡 Tip: Avoid vague problems like "5G is slow." Be specific: "Handover latency in 5G mmWave exceeds 50ms, violating URLLC standards."

1.2. SMART Objectives

Objectives translate your problem into actionable steps. They must be SMART.

Criteria Definition Engineering Example
Specific Clear and unambiguous. What exactly will you do? "Design a beamforming algorithm..." (Not "Study beamforming.")
Measurable Can you quantify the outcome? "...to improve spectral efficiency by 15%."
Achievable Is it realistic given your time/skills? "Using MATLAB simulations..." (Not "Build a satellite.")
Relevant Does it solve the problem stated above? "...to support massive IoT connectivity."
Time-bound Can it be done in the semester/year? "...within the 6-month thesis period."
General Objective: To develop a low-complexity resource allocation scheme for NOMA-based 5G networks.

Specific Objectives:
  1. To model a downlink NOMA system with imperfect CSI in MATLAB.
  2. To propose a user-pairing algorithm that maximizes sum-rate.
  3. To compare the proposed algorithm against OMA and existing NOMA schemes in terms of BER and throughput.
💡 Tip: Do not put your results in the Introduction. Save the "surprise" for the Results section. The Intro is for setting up the question, not answering it.

2. Methods (Methodology)

"Here is how I studied it."

This section must be detailed enough for another engineer to replicate your work exactly. In Communication Engineering, this is often called "System Model and Simulation Setup."

What to Include:
  • System Model: Diagrams of the network topology, antenna arrays, or signal flow.
  • Mathematical Formulation: Key equations defining the channel model, signal processing steps, or optimization problems.
  • Simulation Parameters: A table listing all constants (e.g., Carrier Frequency: 28 GHz, Bandwidth: 100 MHz, Modulation: 64-QAM).
  • Tools: Software used (MATLAB, NS-3, Python) and hardware (USRP, Spectrum Analyzer).
  • Data Analysis: Statistical tests or performance metrics used (e.g., BER, Throughput, Latency).
💡 Tip: Use a Parameter Table. It is much clearer than listing 20 simulation settings in paragraph text.

3. Results

"Here is what I found."

This section presents the data objectively, without interpretation. Just the facts.

Best Practices for Engineering Results:
  • Visuals First: Use graphs, plots, and tables. A BER vs. SNR curve is worth 1000 words.
  • Label Everything: Axes must have units (e.g., "SNR (dB)", "Throughput (Mbps)"). Legends must be clear.
  • Highlight Key Trends: Point out the most important data points (e.g., "As shown in Fig. 3, the proposed method outperforms the baseline at SNR > 15dB.").
  • No Opinion: Do not say "This result is good." Say "This result is 15% higher than the baseline." Save the "good/bad" judgment for the Discussion.
Do Don't
"Figure 1 shows that latency decreases as bandwidth increases." "Figure 1 proves that our method is amazing."
Report exact p-values or error margins. Say "The results were significantly better" without data.

4. Discussion

"Here is what it means."

This is the most important section for demonstrating your critical thinking. You interpret the results and connect them back to the Introduction.

Key Questions to Answer:
  1. Interpretation: Why did you get these results? (e.g., "The improvement is likely due to the reduced interference from the beamforming step.")
  2. Comparison: How do your results compare to previous studies? (e.g., "Unlike Smith et al., who found X, we found Y. This discrepancy may be due to our use of a more realistic channel model.")
  3. Implications: What does this mean for the field? (e.g., "This suggests that predictive handover is viable for high-speed trains.")
  4. Limitations: What went wrong? What are the constraints? (e.g., "The algorithm requires high computational power, which may limit its use in low-cost devices.")
  5. Future Work: What should be done next?
💡 Tip: Be honest about limitations. Acknowledging weaknesses makes your research look more rigorous, not less.

5. Oral Presentation Tips

Writing a report is different from presenting it. A presentation is a performance.

Aspect Guideline
Slides Minimal text. One idea per slide. Use high-quality diagrams. Font size > 24pt.
Structure Follow IMRaD(Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion) but spend 60% of time on Methods and Results. Keep Intro short.
Delivery Speak slowly. Make eye contact. Do not read from the slides.
Q&A Listen to the whole question. Pause before answering. If you don't know, say "That's an interesting point, I will look into it."
✅ The 10-20-30 Rule (Guy Kawasaki):
  • No more than 10 slides.
  • No longer than 20 minutes.
  • No font smaller than 30 points.
