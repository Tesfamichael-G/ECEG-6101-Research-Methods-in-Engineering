1. Based on Purpose

Research is classified by its ultimate goal: is it to expand knowledge for knowledge's sake, or to solve a specific practical problem?

Type Description Engineering Example
Basic Research
(Pure/Fundamental)
  • Driven by curiosity and the desire to expand knowledge.
  • Focuses on generalizations and formulation of theories.
  • Not necessarily concerned with solving a specific societal problem immediately.
Shannon’s Information Theory (1948):
Claude Shannon developed the mathematical theory of communication without a specific product in mind. It laid the foundation for all modern digital communications.
Applied Research
(Practical)
  • Designed to solve specific, practical problems facing an individual, group, or society.
  • Uses existing theories to find solutions.
  • Often leads to patents, products, or policy changes.
 5G Network Optimization:
Developing a new beamforming algorithm specifically to reduce latency in urban 5G networks by 10ms. The goal is a tangible improvement in service.
Note for M.Sc. Students: Most Master's theses are Applied Research. You are expected to take existing theories and apply them to a new scenario, optimization problem, or system design.

2. Based on Approach (Methodology)

This classification refers to the nature of the data and the methods used to analyze it.

A. Quantitative Research

Focuses on quantifying the collection and analysis of data. It is structured, objective, and statistical.

  • Data: Numbers, metrics, signals, logs.
  • Tools: MATLAB, Python, SPSS, Spectrum Analyzers.
  • Goal: To test hypotheses, look for cause-and-effect, and make predictions.
Comm. Eng. Example: Measuring the Bit Error Rate (BER) of a Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM) scheme at different Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) levels. The result is a numerical curve.

B. Qualitative Research

Focuses on understanding concepts, thoughts, or experiences. It is subjective and interpretive.

  • Data: Words, images, observations, interview transcripts.
  • Tools: Interviews, focus groups, case studies.
  • Goal: To gain an in-depth understanding of underlying reasons, opinions, and motivations.
Comm. Eng. Example: Interviewing telecom engineers to understand the challenges they face when deploying fiber optics in rural areas. The result is a thematic analysis of barriers.

C. Mixed Methods

Combines both quantitative and qualitative approaches to provide a complete picture.

Example: 1. Surveying users about their satisfaction with Wi-Fi speed (Quantitative).
2. Interviewing unhappy users to ask why they are dissatisfied (Qualitative).

3. Based on Nature of Inquiry

Type Definition When to Use?
Exploratory Conducted for a problem that has not been clearly defined. It helps determine the best research design, data collection method, and selection of subjects. When you are entering a completely new field (e.g., "What are the potential security risks of 6G?").
Descriptive Describes the characteristics of the population or phenomenon being studied. It focuses on the "what" rather than the "why." When you need to map out current status (e.g., "Describing the spectrum usage efficiency in City X").
Explanatory (Causal) Attempts to clarify why and how there is a relationship between two or more aspects of a situation or phenomenon. When you want to prove cause-and-effect (e.g., "Does increasing antenna height CAUSE better coverage?").
Empirical Based on observed and measured phenomena and derives knowledge from actual experience rather than from theory or belief. Almost all engineering research. You must have data (simulated or real) to support your claims.

4. Based on Source of Data

Primary Data: First-hand information collected by the researcher specifically for the current research problem.
  • Methods: Experiments, Surveys, Observations, Prototyping.
  • Pros: Specific to your needs, high control, current.
  • Cons: Time-consuming, expensive, requires resources.
  • Eng. Example: Running your own MATLAB simulations or measuring signal strength with a Universal Software Radio Peripheral (USRP) device.
Secondary Data: Data that has already been collected by someone else for a different purpose.
  • Methods: Literature review, government reports, public datasets, company records.
  • Pros: Cheap, fast, large sample sizes available.
  • Cons: May be outdated, may not fit your specific variables, quality unknown.
  • Eng. Example: Using the "IEEE DataPort" dataset for traffic patterns instead of collecting your own.

5. Based on Field of Study

While research methods are universal, the context changes the ethical and practical considerations.

Field Focus Key Considerations
Natural Sciences
(Physics, Chem)		 Laws of nature, matter, energy. High controllability, reproducibility is key.
Engineering
(Your Field)		 Application of science to build solutions. Feasibility, cost, safety standards, optimization.
Social Sciences
(Sociology, Psych)		 Human behavior, societies. Ethics (human subjects), subjectivity, complex variables.
Medical Health, disease, treatment. Strict ethical clearance (IRB), clinical trials, life-critical.
Interdisciplinary Research: Modern Communication Engineering often overlaps with Social Science (e.g., studying the digital divide) or Medical fields (e.g., wearable health monitors). Be aware of the ethical guidelines of the field you are touching.
