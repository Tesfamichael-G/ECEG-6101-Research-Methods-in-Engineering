1. Sources of Knowledge

Before conducting research, it is important to understand how humans acquire knowledge. In engineering, we rely on specific sources while rejecting others for scientific validity.

Source Description Reliability in Engineering
Tradition/Authority Knowledge passed down from experts or textbooks. Medium: Good starting point, but must be verified. Experts can be wrong.
Common Sense "It stands to reason." Intuitive beliefs. Low: Often biased or incorrect in complex systems (e.g., quantum mechanics).
Personal Experience What you have observed yourself. Low: Subjective and limited sample size. Not generalizable.
Scientific Method High: The gold standard. Objective, replicable, and falsifiable.
Engineering Example: Tradition says copper is the best conductor. Research might reveal that graphene has better conductivity under specific conditions. We use research to challenge tradition.

2. Defining Research

Research is a systematic, controlled, empirical, and critical investigation of hypothetical propositions about the presumed relations among natural phenomena.

Key Characteristics of Engineering Research:

  • Systematic: Follows a structured procedure (not haphazard).
  • Controlled: Variables are managed to isolate cause and effect.
  • Empirical: Based on observable evidence (data), not just theory.
  • Critical: Scrutinizes procedures and conclusions for errors.
  • Replicable: Others can repeat the study and get similar results.
Non-Research vs. Research:
  • Non-Research: Building a standard antenna using a known design.
  • Research: Modifying the antenna geometry to improve gain at 60GHz and statistically analyzing the performance improvement against existing models.

3. The Purpose of Research

Why do we conduct research in Communication Engineering?

  1. Exploration: To investigate a new phenomenon (e.g., "How does Terahertz radiation behave in rain?").
  2. Description: To accurately describe characteristics of a situation (e.g., "Mapping signal coverage in urban canyons").
  3. Explanation: To explain why something happens (e.g., "Why does latency increase when network load exceeds 80%?").
  4. Prediction/Control: To predict future outcomes and control variables (e.g., "Developing an algorithm to predict channel fading and compensate for it").

4. Research Method vs. Methodology

These terms are often used interchangeably, but they are distinct.

Feature Research Method Research Methodology
Definition The specific tools, techniques, or procedures used to collect and analyze data. The overarching strategy, rationale, and philosophical framework guiding the research.
Focus How you do it. Why you do it that way.
Examples Surveys, MATLAB simulations, Spectrum Analyzers, t-tests, Interviews. Quantitative Approach, Experimental Design, Positivism, Case Study Strategy.
Analogy: If research is building a house, the Methodology is the architectural plan and engineering principles. The Methods are the hammer, saw, and nails.

5. Philosophy of Research

Research philosophy refers to the system of beliefs about how data should be gathered, analyzed, and used. In Engineering, we primarily lean towards Positivism, but it is important to know the others.

A. Positivism (The Engineering Standard)

  • Belief: Reality is objective, single, and measurable.
  • Goal: To find universal laws and cause-and-effect relationships.
  • Method: Highly structured, quantitative, statistical.
  • Example: Measuring the Bit Error Rate (BER) of a modulation scheme. The BER is a fact, independent of the observer.

B. Interpretivism

  • Belief: Reality is subjective and socially constructed.
  • Goal: To understand human meaning and experience.
  • Method: Qualitative, interviews, observations.
  • Example: Studying how users feel about the privacy implications of 5G tracking. This is subjective.

C. Pragmatism

  • Belief: The "truth" is whatever works best to solve the problem.
  • Method: Mixed Methods (combining Quant and Qual).
  • Example: Developing a new telecom policy by combining technical signal data (Positivism) with user acceptance surveys (Interpretivism).
Which one are you? As an M.Sc. in Communication Engineering student, your thesis will likely be Positivist. You are measuring physical phenomena (signals, noise, data rates) that exist objectively.
